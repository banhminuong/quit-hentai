# Advice to Non-users

## Help your hentai-using friends read this book

First, genuinely understand what the book covers before attempting to share it. Try to put yourself in the position of a person still caught in the habit, rather than approaching the conversation as someone with an answer they need to accept.

Don't lead with health warnings or catastrophising. Users know about the risks better than most people assume. They continue not because they're ignorant of the downsides, but because the conditioning makes them believe they need it, that it relaxes them, gives them a confidence or emotional baseline they couldn't otherwise access, or that real intimacy and ordinary life will always feel slightly flat without it. Telling someone their habit is ruining them tends to produce defensiveness, not reflection. Backed into a corner, most people hold their position harder, and the habit can become even more precious as a result.

What actually helps is coming from the other side: connecting them with accounts from people who've gone through this and came out better for it, rather than simply worse off without something they'd lost. Ex-users who genuinely no longer want hentai, who aren't "staying strong" but who simply don't think about it anymore, are far more persuasive than any argument. People who've used the willpower method and describe ongoing struggle are, unintentionally, the opposite of helpful, since they model quitting as permanent deprivation, which only reinforces the fear.

Once the person starts to believe that stopping is genuinely possible and doesn't have to feel like loss, they're likely to be more open to this book. At that point, you can mention that this approach is different from scare-based methods, and that references to health effects are a small fraction of the material. Let them arrive at the book themselves rather than pushing it on them.

Don't approach the conversation as a debate to win. If you push too hard or too often, you'll push them away from the message along with yourself.

## Should I tell my significant other?

There's no single right answer, and it depends heavily on what's already happened.

If you've previously tried to quit using willpower and your partner is already aware of the habit, it makes sense to tell them about this approach. They can read the book if they want to understand the method, and they'll be in a much better position to support you through the withdrawal period than if they don't know what's happening.

If you're only now realising the extent of the habit and have never discussed it, try the method yourself first. For most people, this is an easier process than expected, and if it goes smoothly, there may be no particular reason to revisit a difficult conversation. That said, if you're finding it genuinely hard, being open and honest with a partner can strengthen the relationship rather than damage it.

If the habit was never causing obvious friction, and you're moving through the process relatively easily, it's fine to simply let it resolve without making it a formal conversation. Your partner may notice changes in energy, presence, and connection, and wonder about them. That's generally a good problem to have.

## My partner is quitting hentai

Hentai use, at its more serious stages, tends to produce relationship patterns worth understanding: withdrawal, misdirected irritability, dishonesty about the habit, and sometimes patterns that look more like manipulation when examined closely. Not every user reaches those stages, but dependency doesn't stay static. If you're recognising genuinely concerning patterns in how you've been treated, a therapist with experience in compulsive behaviour is a more appropriate resource than a self-help book.

If your partner is in the withdrawal period, the most useful thing you can do is assume they're finding it harder than they're saying, regardless of how they appear. Don't tell them it's easy. They can tell themselves that. Tell them you've noticed how much better they seem, how much easier they are to be around, how proud you are. Keep saying it, because the early motivation tends to fade faster than most people expect, and consistent encouragement from someone close to them is more sustaining than they'll often admit.

If they're not talking about hentai, don't assume they've stopped thinking about it. Under willpower-based approaches, people usually think about almost nothing else. Raising it gently is fine, they'll tell you if they'd rather not. What they generally won't tell you, but need, is continued acknowledgement that something real is happening and that you've noticed.

Look for ways to make the first weeks genuinely enjoyable rather than just endurable. Interesting evenings, low-pressure situations, moments that feel like relief rather than test. If they become irritable, try not to meet it with irritability. This period is temporary and the irritability, where it exists, is mostly the withdrawal process expressing itself sideways.

One pattern worth knowing: a person quitting through willpower may reach a point of agitation where they seem to be deliberately creating conflict, hoping you'll tell them to just go back to the habit so they don't have to be the one who gave in. If this happens, don't take the bait. Something like: *"I can see this is hard right now. It'll pass. I'm glad you're doing this and I'm not going anywhere."*

Healing from this goes in both directions. Your own support network, your own space to process what this period has been like, and your own boundaries all matter here too. Recovery of trust and genuine connection takes longer than the withdrawal period itself, and that's a real thing to make room for.

## Slipping (relapse)

This section exists as both a resource and a warning. The fact that you're reading it at all means you should take what follows seriously.

Start by adjusting the word. "Relapse" carries a weight of failure and permanence that's usually more damaging than the slip itself. What's actually happened is that you fed the little monster once, which has reactivated it and started the conditioning back up. That's real, but it's not the same as being back at square one.

When people slip, a cluster of specific unhelpful thoughts tends to follow:

- *"I'll never actually be free"* — this is catastrophising. One instance is not a lifetime.
- *"I should have been stronger, I should never have had any urges at all"* — this treats an ordinary human process as a character defect, which makes it worse.
- *"I've already broken the streak so what's the point of stopping now"* — this is the most dangerous one, because it's the thought that turns a single slip into a full return.
- *"Everyone else manages this fine, I'm a hopeless case"* — comparing your insides to other people's outsides.
- *"I shouldn't even be having these thoughts"* — especially common for people whose sense of self or religious identity is tied to sexual behaviour. Worth asking honestly: is self-punishment actually moving you closer to the life you want, or is it just adding suffering?

The conditioning takes time to fully dismantle. Nearly every industry uses sexual imagery, and actively noticing and responding to that requires conscious effort initially. A slip usually means something in the conditioning didn't fully shift, not that the method doesn't work. Rereading the relevant sections is genuinely worthwhile.

A useful thought experiment that some people find helpful: imagine a community dedicated to "not drinking bleach." Members track their clean days, share strategies, occasionally slip and report the struggle. Read accounts from that community and something becomes obvious very quickly: the entire framework treats something poisonous as though it has genuine appeal that needs to be resisted, rather than simply being something nobody actually wants. A slip on bleach-avoidance wouldn't produce guilt and a counter reset. It would produce immediate clarity about why you'd never do that again. The goal is arriving at that same relationship with hentai, not through repression but through genuine understanding that there's nothing there worth having.

You are not your urges. Mindfulness practice, specifically the skill of noticing a thought without acting on it or attaching to it, is genuinely compatible with this method and with most religious traditions. You cannot fight your own mind and win. The approach that works is acceptance combined with clear-eyed understanding of what the urge actually is, not something to obey, but the little monster asking to be fed. Every time you don't feed it, it weakens. Pick yourself up, identify where the understanding broke down, and continue.

## What about MO (masturbation, orgasm)?

Masturbation has been a normal part of human experience across cultures and history. To be direct: hentai is the problem, not masturbation itself.

That said, hentai and masturbation typically become so intertwined that separating them during the early weeks matters practically. Many people find that masturbation during the recovery period triggers hentai-linked fantasies, which can restart the conditioning even without any actual material. As the brain adjusts over time, this tends to resolve on its own. Taking a break from masturbation during the withdrawal period, and prioritising real intimacy where that's available, tends to speed the rewiring along.

There's also a broader question some people arrive at around orgasm frequency generally, separate from hentai. Some find that reducing orgasm frequency, with or without a partner, produces noticeable improvements in energy, focus, and mood. The physiology here is real: orgasm triggers a hormonal response that temporarily reduces dopamine availability, and repeated orgasms in quick succession can produce a flatness that resembles the post-session low familiar from hentai use. Whether and how to engage with this is genuinely personal, and there's no universal prescription. Evaluate it for yourself and notice what actually produces the results you want in your own life.

Some people who've done this describe genuinely better intimate experiences once the pressure of orgasm-as-goal has been removed from sex: more sustained, more connected, more satisfying in a way that doesn't leave either person wanting to be somewhere else immediately afterward. This is worth exploring with a partner if the opportunity arises, and worth considering without pressure if it doesn't.

## Deviations from standard advice

This is worth naming because it comes up more often than might be expected.

Some people, by the time they reach the point of making the final session, find that their desire to actually have one has disappeared. The thought of sitting through hentai one more time feels genuinely unappealing rather than like a sacrifice. **This is completely fine.** If you've already arrived at a genuine sense of freedom before making a formal final session, you don't need to engineer one.

That said, don't underestimate the value of the final session as described if the desire to watch is still present. Going through it consciously, with the understanding you've now built, and closing the browser for the last time with genuine relief rather than suppressed craving, is a real and meaningful way to end the pattern. For many people it's the clearest moment they've had, and remembering it clearly helps during the first weeks.

If you've already been free for a meaningful stretch and have only now come across this book and addressed the underlying conditioning, there's no need to restart anything. The little monster has already left. Feeding it again just to have a formal ending would only give it something to work with. Enjoy the freedom you already have.

## Help end this scandal

Internet hentai dependency is a genuine public health issue that largely gets treated as a personal failing or at most a social curiosity. The truth is that millions of people are caught in a well-constructed trap, one that specifically targets adolescents before they're equipped to recognise what's happening, and that the mechanisms keeping them there are the same ones keeping cigarette smokers, alcohol drinkers, and users of other addictive substances stuck.

The comparison isn't metaphorical. The neurological mechanism is similar, the marketing and distribution systems are optimised to maximise engagement and dependency, and the cultural message, that this is simply adult entertainment and personal choice, is as carefully constructed as any tobacco industry talking point. "We put up a warning, it's your choice" does not constitute meaningful protection when the warning is a speed bump designed to be ignored, there's no age verification that actually works, and the product being offered is, by design, calibrated to produce dependency.

Scare campaigns don't help users stop and they don't stop teenagers from starting. What actually works is genuine understanding of the mechanism, the same understanding this book has been building. Someone who genuinely sees the trap doesn't need willpower to stay out of it. Spreading that understanding is more useful than spreading alarm.

If you know someone struggling with this, pointing them toward this method is a concrete thing you can do. If you encounter the casual normalisation of compulsive hentai use online or in conversation, calm, non-preachy engagement with what's actually happening is more effective than condemnation. You'll occasionally encounter resistance or irritation. Sometimes a single honest comment is all someone needed to start looking at their own situation differently, and you may never know that it made a difference.

The cultural conversation around this is shifting. The people leaving the habit are growing in number, and those still in it can sense the current changing direction. Being visibly free, genuinely better off, and willing to say so plainly when it's relevant, is itself a form of contribution.

## Final Warning

You're now equipped to live the rest of your life as a genuinely happy non-user. To make sure you do:

1. Keep this book accessible and return to any chapter that becomes relevant again. You don't need to re-read from the beginning, go straight to whatever section is most useful.

2. If you ever find yourself envying someone still using, look at them clearly: they're not enjoying it, they're managing a habit they'd rather be free of. You're the one with nothing to envy.

3. You didn't enjoy being a user. That's why you stopped. You enjoy being a non-user. Keep those facts in that order.

4. There is no such thing as just one peek. There never was.

5. Never doubt your decision. You know it's the right one. Doubting it only creates suffering without changing anything real.

6. If you find yourself genuinely struggling beyond what this book addresses, a therapist with experience in compulsive sexual behaviour is the right next step. Finding one through a qualified professional directory is worth the effort.
