## Warning

If you're expecting this book to "scare" you into quitting by listing every health and relationship risk associated with hentai use, such as difficulty with arousal in real-world intimacy, escalating tolerance toward more extreme or novel content, or the discomfort of realising how much time and money has gone into it, alongside the blunt accusation that you're a weak-willed pervert who should simply try harder, you'll be disappointed. Those tactics rarely help anyone quit, and if they were going to work for you, you'd have quit already.

Conventional approaches to quitting hentai tend to fall into one of two camps. The first is willpower: white-knuckling through cravings, blocking software, accountability partners, "just don't look." The second is moderation: switching to "tamer" genres, setting a weekly limit, allowing it only as a reward. Some recovery communities cite real neuroscience about dopamine and novelty-seeking, and that material isn't wrong, but knowing the mechanism isn't the same as being free of the urge. Both willpower and moderation fail for the same underlying reason: they don't remove the *want*. Turning something into a forbidden, rationed treat is not how addiction is actually treated.

This method, EasyPeasy, works differently. Some of what follows might be difficult to believe at first, but by the time you've finished this book, you won't just believe it, you'll wonder how you were ever convinced of the alternative.

There's a common misconception that we choose to watch hentai, the way we might choose a TV show. People dependent on hentai (yes, dependent, even if the word "addict" feels too strong for something that's just pictures and animation) no more choose this than someone dependent on alcohol chooses alcoholism. It's true that we choose to open the browser, choose to type in the site, choose the next chapter or episode. But nobody consciously decided, the first time, to still be doing this years later, organizing parts of their life around it, feeling unable to stop. Curiosity and a normal human interest in sex led most of us there. Nobody would have continued if they'd known, on day one, what it would cost in time, secrecy, and connection to real people.

Take a moment to reflect: did you ever make a deliberate, considered decision that you *need* hentai specifically, more than other forms of intimacy or pleasure, to feel calm, entertained, or aroused? Did you decide, consciously, that real intimacy would feel flat by comparison? At what point did it become something you reached for automatically when stressed, bored, or alone, rather than something you occasionally enjoyed?

Like every other hentai user, you've been drawn into a trap that's subtle precisely because the material doesn't look or feel like "real" pornography. It's stylised. It's often funny, or sweet, or tied to stories and characters people genuinely like. That's part of what makes it dangerous: it doesn't carry the same instinctive sense of "this is something to be careful with" that other pornography might. Almost nobody wants their child relying on hentai to cope or to feel okay. That alone tells you something: nobody needs it before they get hooked, and most people, if asked honestly, wish they'd never started.

And yet, at the same time, every user wants to keep using. Nobody forces the next click. It's only ever the user who decides to open the next chapter, the next episode, the next tab.

If there were a button you could press to wake up tomorrow as if you'd never seen your first piece of hentai, the only people still drawn to it would be those young enough to be encountering it for the first time, out of simple curiosity, before any habit had formed.

The only thing standing between you and quitting is **FEAR**. Fear that you'll have to endure some indefinite stretch of misery, boredom, or unsatisfied craving to be free of it. That fear comes from irrational beliefs, picked up gradually, such as:

- That masturbation to orgasm is the most important and necessary part of unwinding or falling asleep.

- That hentai is "safer" than pursuing real intimacy, because a drawing can't reject you, judge you, or complicate your life.

- That because it's fictional and harms no real performer, it's essentially harmless and not worth questioning.

- That you're entitled to a "perfect," endlessly variable experience that real partners or your own imagination can't match.

- That more variety, more novelty, more extreme content is always better.

Acted on, these beliefs lead to real consequences:

- Chasing increasingly niche or intense content to recreate a feeling that's faded.

- Quietly comparing real partners, or your own sex life, to an idealized and infinitely customisable fictional standard.

- Feeling like something is wrong with you for wanting connection that feels "boring" by comparison.

- Avoiding real intimacy because it requires effort, vulnerability, and the possibility of rejection.

- Forcing yourself through the cycle even when it no longer brings real enjoyment, just relief from not doing it.

It's fear that an evening alone will be unbearable without it. Fear that you won't be able to relax, fall asleep, or get through a stressful day without that release. Fear that your personality, your concentration, your way of unwinding will permanently change without your "harmless" habit.

But most of all, it's the fear that you're simply built this way now, that you'll spend the rest of your life quietly craving it at odd moments, never fully free. If you've already tried cutting down, switching genres, or gritting your teeth through the willpower method, that fear is probably familiar, and you may already be convinced you can't actually quit.

If you're anxious or unsure the timing is right, understand this clearly: that anxiety isn't relieved by hentai, it's caused by the trap hentai has built around you. You didn't choose to fall into it, but like any trap, it's built to keep you there. When you watched your first hentai, did you decide then that you'd still be watching it years on? So when exactly will you stop? Tomorrow? Next year? The trap doesn't work on a deadline you set. That's precisely why so many people drift through years of it without ever consciously deciding to stay.

EasyPeasy works like that imaginary button. It isn't magic. But for the people who've used it, including whoever first adapted it for this purpose, it can feel close to it.

Here is the honest warning: every user wants to quit, and every user can find quitting easy. The only thing in the way is fear, and you won't lose that fear until you finish the book. You didn't decide to fall into this trap, but you do have to decide, actively, to leave it. You may already want out badly, or you may feel unsure about even reading this. ### A note on what this book is, and isn't

This book uses a behavioural and cognitive approach to address compulsive hentai use. It is not a substitute for medical care, psychiatric treatment, or legal advice, and it does not diagnose any condition. If your use of hentai (or any pornography) is tangled up with depression, trauma, OCD, or compulsive behaviour that feels outside your control in ways this book doesn't address, please also consider speaking with a doctor or therapist alongside reading this.

One further point needs to be stated plainly, separate from everything else in this book. If your viewing habits have ever drifted toward content depicting characters who appear to be minors, this is not a "genre preference" to work through with willpower or habit-change techniques, and this book is not the right tool for it. That pattern needs a conversation with a qualified mental health professional, not a self-help method. If this applies to you, please reach out to a therapist or a relevant professional support service rather than trying to handle it alone.

## Finally

Everyone can find it easy and enjoyable to quit hentai, including you. All you have to do is read the rest of this book with an open mind; the more you understand, the easier it becomes. Even if a section doesn't fully land on first read, provided you follow the instructions, you'll still find it easy. Most importantly, you won't spend your life missing hentai or feeling deprived. By the end of the book, the real mystery will be why it took this long.

With EasyPeasy, there are only two reasons for failure.

**Failure to follow instructions.**
Some readers find it frustrating that this book is so insistent about a few specific things, like not trying to cut down first, or not reaching for "safer" substitute genres. People do occasionally succeed despite ignoring that advice, but they succeed in spite of it, not because of it. The combination for this particular lock is in this book, but the numbers have to go in order: chapter by chapter, without skipping ahead.

**Failure to understand.**
Don't take anything here for granted. Question what you've been told, by society, by communities, by your own past justifications, about hentai, fiction, and addiction. If you believe it's "just a habit," ask why some genuinely enjoyable habits are easy to drop while this one, which often leaves you feeling worse afterward, isn't. If you believe you simply enjoy it, ask why you don't feel the same pull toward other things you enjoy far more. Why does this specifically have to happen? Why does skipping it cause unease?

This book exists to show you how easy and enjoyable quitting hentai can be. Escaping it is something every user would value, whether or not they'd admit that out loud right now.
