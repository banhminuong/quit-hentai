## REBT Coping Statements

*REBT (Rational Emotive Behaviour Therapy) is a form of cognitive-behavioural therapy developed by Albert Ellis. The following coping statements are adapted for hentai recovery. PMO refers to the cycle of (h)entai, Masturbation, and Orgasm.*

- *"I can stop PMO, even though it appears 'hard' to do so. It's not too hard, and no matter how much trouble it takes, it's worth it."*

- *"If I keep ignoring and never giving in to my urges to PMO, I will make it easier and easier to resist them."*

- *"I can fully and unconditionally accept myself — even with all my flaws and failings."*

- *"PMO seems to quickly 'cure' my problems, but actually makes them worse."*

- *"At times I'd like very much to drown my troubles in PMO, but that is never a reason to do so."*

- *"It's uncomfortable when I don't get what I want. But it's not awful or terrible unless I choose to believe that it is, and I can choose to believe something more realistic and helpful instead."*

- *"I'll never like unfair treatment, but I can stand it and do something about it."*

- *"No matter how many times I fail at this, my failure never makes me an incompetent person. It just makes me a person who acted incompetently in that moment."*

- *"I don't absolutely need what I want, but I can still be reasonably happy without it."*

- *"Many things can make me sorry and disappointed, but when I demand that these things must not exist, I make myself panicked, depressed, and enraged."*

- *"Yes, I've often failed to do what I promised I'd do. But that doesn't mean I can't carry out this promise now."*

- *"I hate being anxious and depressed, but I don't have to immediately dissolve those feelings with PMO. When I PMO, I temporarily feel better about my problems but I don't actually get better. In the long run, PMO makes things worse."*

- *"People don't make me angry by treating me badly. I choose to make myself angry about their bad treatment by demanding that they act better."*

## Combining EasyPeasy with Jack Trimpey's Addictive Voice Recognition Technique (AVRT)

*This section was contributed by a community member and is included here because it addresses something EasyPeasy on its own occasionally leaves people without: a practical framework for handling the little monster directly, for those who find that the physical craving lingers even after the conditioning has been genuinely addressed.*

---

This is for people who are struggling to use the EasyPeasy method to recover despite having genuinely removed the brainwashing. It assumes you've read and understood EasyPeasy. It would also help to read *Rational Recovery* by Jack Trimpey, which covers AVRT in much greater depth than what follows here. This isn't aimed at hentai specifically and applies to any dependency.

The purpose is to compare EasyPeasy with AVRT and explain how combining them can be the missing piece for people who fail through EasyPeasy alone despite having genuinely cleared the conditioning.

---

**The two methods compared**

EasyPeasy and AVRT are both effective, but they target different things.

EasyPeasy divides the addiction into the Little Monster (the physical craving) and the Big Monster (the conditioning: the beliefs that the habit provides something real). EasyPeasy focuses almost entirely on dismantling the Big Monster. Once that's done, the theory goes, the Little Monster withers on its own since it has no psychological oxygen to feed on.

AVRT, developed by Jack Trimpey, divides the brain into the lower brain (the limbic system, where addiction lives) and the higher brain (the prefrontal cortex, where rational thought and decision-making live). The "Addictive Voice" (what EasyPeasy calls the Little Monster) lives in the lower brain. It can't directly control your behaviour, but it can hijack your inner voice to try to convince you to give it what it wants.

These two frameworks are actually complementary. AVRT is essentially a technique for handling the Little Monster directly. EasyPeasy makes it unnecessary in theory, but in practice, some people continue to experience a residual pull even after the Big Monster is genuinely gone, and AVRT gives them a way to understand and work with that.

---

**How the Addictive Voice works**

The Addictive Voice (AV) hides behind the pronoun "I." When you're still fully in the addiction, it says things like:

- *"I could really use a session right now."*
- *"I miss that feeling."*
- *"Wouldn't it be nice, just this once? I deserve it after the day I've had."*

This is why people mistake the AV for themselves. It uses their own internal voice, their own first-person perspective, to make its demands. Most people don't realise the voice isn't them.

When you say "No" to the AV, something revealing happens. It drops the "I" and shifts to "you," "us," or "we":

- *"Oh come on, you really could use this right now."*
- *"We deserve this. How could you deny us?"*
- *"You're going to give in eventually anyway."*

This shift is significant. It's proof that the voice isn't you. You are the one saying "No." The AV is the one arguing. They're not the same entity.

---

**This isn't the tug-of-war Allen Carr describes**

The tug-of-war in EasyPeasy refers to cognitive dissonance, the internal conflict that arises when you still believe (on some level) that the habit offers something real. That's the Big Monster. "I know it's bad for me, but it also helps me relax, so I want it."

Once the Big Monster is genuinely dead, that cognitive dissonance disappears. What remains, if anything does, is the AV, the Little Monster making its case without the support of a belief system. Because the AV still uses "I," it's easy to mistake it for remaining Big Monster activity. That mistake can send you back to rereading the book looking for conditioning you haven't fully cleared, when in fact the conditioning is gone and what you're dealing with is simply the physical craving wearing your voice like a costume.

---

**Why misidentifying the AV causes problems**

EasyPeasy says there are two reasons for failure: not following instructions, or not fully removing the brainwashing. This is almost always true, but it leaves one gap: some people have genuinely removed the brainwashing and are still experiencing the AV, and because EasyPeasy's only explanation for failure is "the Big Monster isn't fully dead," they conclude they must not have cleared the conditioning yet, and go back to rereading rather than addressing what's actually happening.

This can create a cycle: read the book, clear the conditioning, encounter the AV, misidentify it as residual conditioning, read the book again, repeat.

The test is simple. When you hear a voice arguing for the habit, say "No" clearly. If it shifts from "I" to "you" or "we," that's the AV, not the Big Monster. The Big Monster wouldn't make that shift. Recognising this should give you clarity about what you're actually dealing with.

---

**Emotions from the AV**

When you say "No" to the AV, it may produce an emotional response: fear, sadness, a sense of loss, urgency. This is important to understand. These feelings are coming from the AV, not from you. They're its last-ditch attempt to get what it wants by making you feel like you're the one suffering.

If you can't distinguish the AV from yourself, you'll experience these emotions as your own and be more likely to give in. If you can recognise them as the AV's, you can observe them, name them for what they are, and let them pass without acting on them.

There's something worth experiencing in this moment: a kind of clear-eyed amusement. The AV is making its case as urgently as it can, using your own voice, producing your own feelings, and the whole thing is essentially a performance by a part of your brain that can't actually make you do anything. You're watching it happen. You're not inside it.

---

**Using both methods together**

For most people, EasyPeasy is sufficient. Once the Big Monster is genuinely dead, the Little Monster fades on its own without much trouble.

For the people for whom it doesn't, AVRT provides the additional tool. The combination works like this:

1. Use EasyPeasy to dismantle the belief that hentai offers something real. Kill the Big Monster.
2. When residual pull appears, use AVRT to recognise it clearly as the AV, not as you, and not as evidence that the conditioning isn't fully gone.
3. Say "No" and watch for the shift from "I" to "you" or "we" as confirmation.
4. Observe any emotional response from the AV without identifying with it.
5. Maintain the feeling of freedom rather than the feeling of suppression. You're not white-knuckling anything. You're just watching something that has no real power over you make noise.

Neither method requires willpower in the traditional sense. Both, in their different ways, are about seeing clearly what's actually happening, which is almost always sufficient.
