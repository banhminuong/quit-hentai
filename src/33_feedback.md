# Feedback

This isn't a campaign against users. It's a campaign against the trap, and it's worth waging because seeing someone genuinely escape from it is one of the more satisfying things there is. But that satisfaction has come with a consistent frustration, mainly from two categories of people.

The first, despite everything in the previous chapter, are the ones who find stopping relatively easy, and then, months or sometimes years later, find themselves back in it and unable to get out as cleanly the second time. It's a bit like finding someone in genuine danger, helping them get clear, and then watching them walk back into the same situation half a year later. ***If you've escaped, please don't make this mistake.*** The logic behind it usually sounds like: *"I can handle this now. I've proven I can stop. If I get caught again, I'll just stop again."* That logic is wrong. Stopping is genuinely easy; controlling the addiction is not possible. The one thing that makes staying free simple is not going back at all.

People usually restart because they've misunderstood what "easy" means in this context. Here's a useful way to think about it. Imagine sitting on the cold concrete floor of a freezing room. On the wall in front of you is a window showing a perfect spring day outside. Right next to it is an unlocked door. Does it take willpower to get up and walk out? No. Is leaving harder than staying? No, it's actually marginally easier because your body is already in motion once you start. So why would anyone sit in that room for months or years making themselves miserable, unless they'd somehow been convinced that outside was dangerous and inside was comfortable? That's exactly what this method has been doing: pulling back the curtain so you can see what's actually outside, and dismantling the belief that the room is somehow better.

The second frustrating category is people who are too frightened to make the attempt at all, or who struggle significantly when they do. The main difficulties tend to cluster around a few specific problems.

**Fear of failure.** There's no real shame in failing. Not trying at all, though, guarantees the outcome you're afraid of. The worst possible result of attempting to quit is that you end up back where you started, no worse off than before. The best possible result is enormous. If you don't try, you've already chosen the worst outcome.

**Fear of pain and misery.** What genuinely awful thing would happen to you if you never watched hentai again? Nothing. The panic that sometimes arrives at the moment of quitting is itself a dopamine response, and it passes quickly. The greatest gain in this whole process is simply being free of that fear. If you find yourself panicking during the early days, deep, deliberate breathing helps. If other people are adding to the pressure, remove yourself from the situation briefly, go somewhere quiet. If you feel like crying, don't fight it. Crying is a real physical mechanism for releasing tension, and no one has ever cried properly and felt worse for it afterward. If you need to shout, shout. Think of what you're going through as a short, intense fight that you cannot actually lose. Nobody can stop time. Every moment that passes, the little monster weakens further. Your victory is already inevitable.

**Not following the instructions.** Some people report that the method didn't work for them and then describe, on closer examination, having set aside most of the instructions rather than following them. The checklist at the end of this chapter summarises what needs to be in place for this to work.

**Misunderstanding the instructions.** Several specific misunderstandings come up repeatedly, and it's worth addressing each one directly.

*"I can't stop thinking about hentai."*
Don't try to. Attempting to force a thought out of your head creates a specific psychological problem, the harder you push it away, the more it comes back. It doesn't matter how often hentai comes to mind during the first few days. What matters is what you do with the thought when it arrives. If the thought arrives and produces *"I wish I could,"* you'll be miserable. If it arrives and produces *"I don't have to do that anymore,"* you'll be fine. Train the second response, not the suppression of the thought.

*"When will the craving actually go?"*
The dopamine response fades very quickly after the final session, but the mild, empty, slightly restless feeling of dopamine withdrawal is essentially identical to the feeling of ordinary hunger or stress. Hentai was only ever amplifying a background level of discomfort that's always present. Because of this, people using willpower alone often can't tell whether they've "kicked it" even when they have, because the mild baseline discomfort continues regardless. You don't need to wait for that feeling to disappear before living freely. When you leave a dentist after a procedure, does your jaw stop aching before you walk out of the building and get on with your day? No, you just get on with it regardless. Do the same here.

*"The moment of revelation hasn't arrived."*
If you're waiting for it, you're generating a new source of doubt that doesn't need to exist. A useful story: someone who had gone three weeks without hentai using willpower mentioned this to an old friend who asked how they were doing. *"I've survived three weeks,"* they said. The friend didn't understand. *"Survived? What do you mean?"* When they explained, the friend said, *"What are you waiting for? You've already done it. You're a non-user."* That was exactly right. The mistake was treating it as a period to survive rather than a state they'd already entered. You become a non-user the moment you close the browser for the last time. The important thing is to be a happy one from the start rather than a miserable one waiting for some further confirmation.

*"I'm still craving it."*
Then look at what you're actually saying. If you say you want hentai, you're saying you want to be a user. You already know you don't want to be a user. That's why you're here. A non-user doesn't want to visit those sites. You know what you want to be, so stop creating a contradiction. The craving is the little monster, not you. Stop feeding it by treating it as if it represents something you genuinely want.

*"I feel like I've opted out of life."*
You haven't opted out of anything. You've stopped doing one specific thing that was quietly working against you. Everything else continues. For the next couple of days, your body will register the mild discomfort of reduced dopamine flooding, the same discomfort you've been carrying in the background throughout this entire habit, every time you were asleep, in a meeting, out with people, anywhere you weren't actively in a session. You've simply stopped adding to it. That discomfort was always there. Now it leaves rather than cycling back around. Social occasions are still good. Meals are still enjoyable. Life is still worth showing up for, and it's about to become noticeably better. Go to things. Be present. You're not the person being deprived, everyone still in the trap is. Every one of them would choose your position over their own if they genuinely understood what they were choosing. There's no reason to envy anyone still caught in it.

*"I'm miserable and irritable."*
This usually means something hasn't landed properly. Go back through the checklist and identify which instruction isn't sitting right. Some people understand everything intellectually and still start with a sense of dread, as if something bad is happening. Nothing bad is happening. Something good is happening. You're doing something that every person still in this trap wishes they could do. The frame of mind you're trying to arrive at, the one where every thought about hentai lands as genuine relief rather than loss, doesn't have to wait for the withdrawal to end. It can start right now. In fact, the only way it works is if it starts right now, because waiting for conditions to feel better before feeling better is a loop that doesn't close.

*"I had a clean stretch but I'm back in it now."*
Remember: fear itself is the pang. Giving in to it feeds the little monster and reinforces the belief that you're more dependent than you actually are. In reality, your understanding of the trap hasn't changed, only the dopamine has returned to the thought process. This is falling forward, not falling back to the beginning. Find which instruction below broke down, understand why, and continue.

## The Checklist

Follow these and you cannot fail:

1. Make a firm, unconditional commitment that you will not watch hentai again, in any form, including static images, adjacent material, ecchi or anything else that engages the same novelty-seeking response. Hold to this without exception.

2. Get this genuinely clear, not just intellectually but in felt experience: there is nothing real being given up here. Not just that life will be better without it, though it will, and not just that there's no rational reason to keep doing it, though there isn't. What's meant is that the pleasure and relief you thought you were getting were never actually coming from hentai. They were the ending of discomfort that hentai created. Like stopping hitting your head against a wall: the good feeling isn't a gift from the wall. It's simply what was always there underneath.

3. There's no such thing as a person who's irreversibly dependent on hentai. You're one of many hundreds of millions who fell for a subtle and well-constructed trap. Like every other person who's gotten free, you are already out.

4. Any time you weigh this up honestly, the conclusion is always the same: there is nothing on the hentai side of the ledger worth keeping. Nothing will ever change that. The decision you've made is the right one. Don't torture yourself by doubting it. There's nothing to lose by staying free, and the gains are real.

5. Don't try not to think about hentai. When you do think about it, at any point today, tomorrow, or years from now, let the thought land as: *"I don't have to do that anymore."*

6. Don't use substitutes. Don't keep devices or material nearby as a test of willpower. Don't avoid ordinary life, films, social situations, conversations, because they might trigger something. Living your life is not a risk to your recovery; it's the point of it. Don't restructure everything around the absence of hentai.

7. Don't wait for the moment of revelation. Get on with your life. Enjoy the good days and handle the bad ones. The moment will arrive on its own, and it usually arrives sooner than expected, and in a quiet, ordinary situation you barely noticed yourself getting through.
