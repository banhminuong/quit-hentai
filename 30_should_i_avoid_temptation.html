Should I Avoid Temptation Situations?

Most of the guidance in this book has been direct, meant to be followed as instruction rather than taken as suggestion. There are real reasons for that approach, backed by a large amount of accumulated experience. On this particular question, however, there's no single right answer. Every person will need to work out their own position. Two useful frameworks for thinking it through are offered below.

The fear that keeps people using hentai for years, long after they've recognised the trap, comes in two distinct phases. Both are worth understanding before you encounter them.

Phase One — "How will I cope without it?"

This is the panicky feeling that shows up when you're on the verge of actually stopping: a sense that you won't be able to manage, that something essential is being removed. It often arrives when you're already under some kind of relational stress, when connection with a real partner feels unavailable or complicated, or when being alone feels harder than usual. Paradoxically, this fear peaks at the moment when your withdrawal pangs are actually at their lowest, right at the point of quitting. The panic isn't caused by the withdrawal itself. It's a psychological fear of dependency, the belief that you can't function without this, showing up as a feeling rather than as a fact.

A useful comparison: the first time someone climbs a diving board. The board looks much higher than it is. The water looks much shallower. It takes real effort to push off, convinced for a moment that something will go wrong. The launch itself is the hardest part. Once you've done it, the rest is simply water. This explains why people who are generally strong-willed, who handle other difficulties in life with no problem, can still find themselves unable to get past that first moment of actually stopping. Some people, having made the decision to stop, immediately binge more intensely than they had before the decision, not despite it. The decision produces panic, the panic triggers the familiar cue, and suddenly all of that is happening faster and more powerfully than usual.

If that's happening or has happened, don't worry, and don't read it as evidence that you're unusually stuck. The panic is psychological. It's the fear of dependency, not dependency itself. Even while still addicted, you aren't as helpless as the panic suggests. Launch yourself anyway.

Phase Two — Longer-Term Fear

The second phase is quieter and more sustained: the fear that certain situations you used to associate with hentai, relaxing alone, certain times of night, stress at work, social occasions where you feel out of step, won't feel manageable or enjoyable anymore without the old fallback. This fear tends to arrive a little later, in the first days and weeks after stopping rather than right at the moment of decision.

Trust the process on this one. If you've genuinely launched yourself past Phase One, you'll find Phase Two resolves faster than you expect, usually by simply experiencing those situations and discovering that you got through them fine, and often better than expected.

On keeping an option available. One version of avoidance looks like this: "I'll stop, but I'll allow myself one session if things get genuinely difficult. I'll set a schedule, once every few days, and if I stick to it that still counts as success." The failure rate for this approach is considerably higher than simply stopping entirely. The reason is straightforward: if you're already in a difficult moment and you've given yourself pre-authorised permission to use hentai under sufficiently hard circumstances, the little monster is very good at deciding that this moment qualifies. A clear, unambiguous rule with no exceptions is actually easier to maintain than a rule with a safety valve built in, because the safety valve is always available to be rationalised open. And if you do end up breaking a rule that had exceptions built into it, you've technically only bent the rule rather than broken it, which makes it easier to continue bending it. The pang you were trying to manage would almost certainly have passed on its own if you'd simply waited.

There are two things that actually determine success here, and both need to be genuinely present, not just intellectually agreed with:

  • Certainty. Not hoping you'll stay stopped, but knowing you are.
  • A sense that this is good news, not bad. Not enduring the absence of something valuable, but recognising there's nothing of value being lost.

If either of those isn't in place yet, something hasn't fully landed. Go back and reread the relevant sections. The conditioning needs to be genuinely dismantled first, not managed around.

On avoiding triggering situations. There's a real distinction worth drawing here.

For genuinely stressful situations, places, contexts, or people that have historically been associated with using hentai and that will put real pressure on you, the advice is yes, give yourself some space during the withdrawal period if you reasonably can. There's no value in deliberately stacking pressure onto an already active process.

For social situations, evenings out, time with friends, ordinary enjoyable events, the advice is the opposite: go, and go immediately. Don't postpone your life until after the withdrawal period has passed. You don't need hentai, or orgasm specifically, to enjoy an evening out, and you didn't even while you were still dependent. Going out and being fully present in an ordinary social situation, without the background pull of "something I need to get back to," is one of the most direct ways to start experiencing the actual freedom that comes with having stopped. You'll find that the evenings that felt like they required hentai as some kind of culmination were never actually about hentai. They were good, or not, on their own terms. Once the little monster has finished fading, those same evenings will feel even better, without the particular pressure of the habit sitting underneath them.
